Abstract
Candida albicans possesses confirmed receptors or binding proteins for estrogen, luteinizing hormone, corticosteroids, androgens, and acetylcholine, plus environmental sensors for glucose, lactate, methionine, temperature, CO₂, pH, amino acids, and oxygen tension. It modulates host dopaminergic signaling, diverts tryptophan from serotonin synthesis, competes for arachidonic acid at the prostaglandin/endocannabinoid branch point, triggers mast cell degranulation, and governs endocannabinoid tone. Through pituitary colonization and endocannabinoid-mediated signaling, it has indirect access to every endocrine axis the pituitary controls. This receptor and modulatory inventory maps mechanically onto dozens of conditions whose textbook descriptions include "unknown etiology" or "multifactorial," including depression, autoimmune disease, obesity, migraine, fibromyalgia, PCOS, endometriosis, dysautonomia, chronic fatigue, irritable bowel syndrome, and substance use disorders. Conservative aggregation of U.S. cost-of-illness estimates across these categories exceeds $2 trillion annually, representing approximately 40% of the nation's $5.3 trillion in total healthcare expenditure. 
This paper maps each organism interface to its associated condition cluster, quantifies the economic burden by category, and argues that the current therapeutic paradigm of treating these conditions as independent diseases constitutes the largest misallocation of medical resources in history. A game-theoretic analysis demonstrates that after approximately one million years of coevolution, the host-organism fitness coupling is obligate: elimination of the organism produces iatrogenic harm, while unmanaged colonization produces the chronic disease burden documented here. The only viable strategy is management of the phase transitions that reset colonization density to levels where maintenance functions operate without pathological outputs overwhelming host compensatory capacity. Under managed conditions, the organism's maintenance functions — glucose regulation, cholesterol management, immune calibration, cardiovascular governance, infection suppression — produce the physiological platform designated Homo Candidus. The cadaveric pituitary growth hormone archive (Jaunmuktane 2015; Purro 2018; Banerjee 2024), conventionally read as evidence of prion-like Aβ transmission, is reinterpreted under the framework as iatrogenic transfer of stuck-state Candida from donor to recipient, with Aβ functioning as host antimicrobial encapsulation residue rather than the transmissible agent. The reading generates an empirical fork testable with existing methodology and a donation-screening implication that extends beyond Aβ assays to organism burden and colonization-state markers across the broader biological supply chain.
The organism's program is designed to maintain the host, not destroy it, and the longitudinal case study (Craddock, Case Study) documents over thirty years of survival in this state without a single physician recognizing the condition. The conditions that currently define the aging trajectory — cardiovascular disease, metabolic deterioration, immune failure, neurodegeneration — are consequences of unmanaged program advancement, not inevitable features of aging. If phase transition intervals can be maintained, the implications for human longevity are measured not in years but in decades. This is not the first time the thread was found; a 1960s UK research corridor converging on the same relationship was dismantled and buried (Craddock, Exposé). The difference now is that the data are published, the framework is citable, and the archive is distributed beyond the reach of any single institution.

The Biological Supply Chain
One demonstrable consequence of unmanaged colonization that the framework can document with direct human evidence has been sitting in the neurology literature for a decade without being read as what it is.
Between 1959 and 1985, approximately 30,000 people worldwide received human growth hormone extracted from cadaveric pituitary glands as treatment for childhood short stature. The program ended when recipients began developing iatrogenic Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease, traced to prion contamination in the extraction lots. Decades later, an unexpected finding emerged in the surviving recipients. Jaunmuktane et al. (2015) reported moderate to severe Aβ parenchymal deposition and cerebral amyloid angiopathy in four of eight iatrogenic CJD patients aged 36 to 51, all of whom had received cadaver-derived pituitary growth hormone in childhood. The Aβ deposition did not co-localize with the prion pathology. It was distinct, and it was AD-pattern. Purro et al. (2018) confirmed the causal chain biochemically by demonstrating that archived c-hGH vials still contained substantial Aβ peptide with seeding capacity in mouse models. Banerjee et al. (2024) reported the first premortem clinical evidence: eight c-hGH recipients developed dementia and AD biomarker changes three to four decades after pituitary growth hormone treatment, establishing iatrogenic AD as a clinical entity in the published record.
This finding has been read, in the AD literature, as confirmation of a prion-like mechanism for Aβ. The dominant interpretation runs as follows. Aβ behaves like a transmissible seed. Donor pituitaries contained Aβ for incidental reasons related to subclinical AD pathology in older individuals. The Aβ co-extracted with the hormone, was injected into children, and templated host-protein misfolding in recipient brains over the decades that followed. The mechanism is treated as analogous to classical prion disease, with Aβ acting as the conformational template. This is now standard reading among researchers engaged with the c-hGH archive, and the screening implication being pursued is that donor biological products should be assayed for Aβ seeding capacity. [The framework reads the c-hGH archive as a population-level signal, not a curiosity.]
The framework agrees that pituitary tissue from donors transferred AD-relevant pathology to recipients. The framework disagrees about what the transmissible agent was, why the donor pituitaries contained Aβ, and what the screening implication should be.
What the framework predicts the donors had: chronic Candida colonization that had engaged the pituitary as a governance control point, a process that develops across decades of unmanaged organism activity and is documented across this corpus. Aβ deposition in donor pituitaries was not incidental subclinical AD pathology. It was the host's antimicrobial encapsulation response (Soscia et al., 2010) to pituitary-localized organism activity. The Aβ was the wrapper. The organism was the message.
What the framework predicts was transferred: organism material co-extracted with the hormone, in a state already configured for pituitary engagement, because it came from a pituitary that was already engaged. Whether this was viable organism, cell-wall fragments, extracellular vesicles, or hyphal residue is a question for empirical work that has not been performed, but the framework's claim is that organism material rather than Aβ peptide was the active payload. The recipients were inoculated with stuck-state Candida, configured at extraction by donor architecture, delivered in childhood, given decades to advance its program in a new host. The recipients developed AD because they received the organism in the state that produces AD. [The Candida transferred was stuck running in a programmatic state that would cause Alzheimer’s Disease]
This produces a clean empirical fork between the two readings. The prion-like model predicts that purified Aβ from donor material, transferred to a model system, reproduces the AD-pathology trajectory, and that Aβ-removed donor material does not. Purro 2018 has already established that Aβ from c-hGH vials seeds Aβ deposition in mice. The symmetric experiment, which would distinguish the two models, has not been performed: transfer of organism material from donor pituitary tissue with Aβ removed or denatured, into a model system, to test whether AD-trajectory pathology still develops. The framework predicts it does. The prion-like model predicts it does not. Either result moves the field.
The donation-screening implication cannot be settled at the level of Aβ seeding assays. If Aβ is the host wrapper around an organism payload, then screening donor biological products for Aβ seeding capacity will detect a subset of high-burden donors while missing the larger population whose colonization architecture has not yet generated detectable encapsulation residue. The Logic of Obligate Partnership
The organism that kills its host dies. This is not a philosophical position. It is an evolutionary constraint. A million years of coevolution have selected, at the population level, for organism lineages whose management programs maintain host viability. Organism variants that consumed too aggressively, that advanced their programs too rapidly, that destroyed host organ systems before completing their own reproductive objectives — those lineages ended when their hosts ended. What remains is an organism whose entire computational architecture is oriented toward keeping the host alive and functional for as long as the organism requires.
The inverse is equally binding. A host that eliminates the organism loses every maintenance function the organism provides. After a million years of coupled selection, human physiology is not merely tolerant of the organism's presence. It is dependent on it. The glucose regulation, immune calibration, cardiovascular governance, cholesterol management, and infection suppression described throughout this framework are not functions the host performs independently with the organism as an unwelcome passenger. They are functions the coupled system performs together. Remove the organism, and the host must perform them alone — with machinery that has been co-opted, shared, or partially offloaded to the symbiont across evolutionary time.
This is what an ideal symbiont looks like from the perspective of evolutionary game theory. Not an organism that helps by choice. An organism that cannot afford to harm, coupled with a host that cannot afford to eliminate. Neither party can defect. The fitness of each is bound to the fitness of the other.
If the host's genetics decline — if metabolic function degrades, if immune competence erodes, if organ systems fail — the organism's environment degrades with them. The organism has every evolutionary incentive to prevent this. If the organism is eliminated — if colonization is eradicated through aggressive antifungal therapy or immune reconstitution — the host loses every maintenance subsidy the organism was providing. The decline that follows is not recovery. It is the unmasking of dependence.
